Ajinomoto, Controlling 95% of ABF Film Market, Hikes Prices 30% as AI Chip Packaging Upgrades Spark Substrate Cost Surge

Ajinomoto, the dominant global supplier of ABF (Ajinomoto Build-up Film) with over 95% market share, has announced a 30% price increase effective Q3 2026. This hike, combined with similar 30% increases on copper-clad laminate materials previously announced by Resonac and Mitsubishi Gas Chemical, is placing full-scale cost pressure on the IC substrate supply chain. AI chip packaging layers are evolving from 3+3 to 11+11 and ultimately 13+13 configurations, pushing ABF substrates back into shortage territory since the first half of 2026. Taiwan-based substrate manufacturers Unimicron and Kinsus Interconnect Technology have already raised their 2026 capital expenditure plans to meet surging demand, with the industry anticipating a super-expansion cycle over the next two to three years. Ajinomoto also announced a ¥1.2 billion (approximately $7.6 million) investment in a third ABF plant in Gifu Prefecture, targeting production by 2032 to meet long-term demand growth.
Key Elements

Global ABF (Ajinomoto Build-up Film) monopoly giant Ajinomoto has officially announced a 30% price increase on its core product, effective Q3 2026. The decision has rapidly ignited cost pressures across the IC substrate supply chain, compounding earlier synchronized price hikes on copper-clad laminate (CCL) materials from Japan's Resonac and Mitsubishi Gas Chemical (MGC) . The entire upstream packaging substrate sector is now experiencing a rare, broad-based wave of price increases. Multiple Taiwan-based IC substrate manufacturers have confirmed receiving official notification from Ajinomoto, and while new pricing remains under difficult negotiation with the supplier, cost pass-through is now unavoidable.

This price hike arrives at a critical juncture—AI chip demand is expanding faster than expected, pushing ABF substrates back into a shortage since the first half of 2026. The industry broadly expects quarterly price increases for both ABF and BT substrates to persist through year-end, with more pronounced adjustments in the second half. For IC substrate makers such as Ibiden (4062.T) , Samsung Electro-Mechanics (009150.KS) , AT &S, and Taiwan's Unimicron (3037.TW) , Kinsus Interconnect Technology (3189.TW) , and Nanya PCB (8046.TW) , the tight supply-demand environment may erode margins in the short term, but over the medium to long term, it is expected to support continued improvement in order visibility.

Upstream Cost Pressures Erupt on Multiple Fronts

Ajinomoto's price hike is not an isolated event but the latest chapter in an across-the-board cost escalation for the IC substrate supply chain. Prior to this, Japan's Resonac and Mitsubishi Gas Chemical had already raised prices on CCL-related materials by 30%, effective April 1, 2026. Industry sources indicate that the greatest cost pressure on IC substrates currently stems from the continued rise in upstream CCL material prices. Ajinomoto's follow-on increase further intensifies the cost burden along the entire chain.

Sources inside IC substrate manufacturers note that the last ABF film price increase occurred roughly one year ago, in early 2025. Given the current strong customer pull-in demand, the 30% magnitude of this hike is still considered "within a reasonable range." Notably, however, supply chain tightness is not limited to ABF film itself. Upstream materials such as glass fabric, copper foil, and drill bits are likewise facing shortages. With multiple bottlenecks compounding, the ABF supply-demand gap is expected to widen further in 2027 and 2028.

Ajinomoto's Absolute Pricing Power and Long-Term Capacity Plans

Ajinomoto commands over 95% of the global ABF market, giving it unassailable pricing authority across the entire supply chain. Unlike Nittobo, a glass fabric supplier whose conservative capacity expansion has resulted in T-glass supply tightness, Ajinomoto initiated capacity expansion more than three years ago, positioning itself ahead of shifts in the ABF substrate supply-demand landscape. Consequently, despite its extremely entrenched monopoly, this dominance has not evolved into a major shipment bottleneck for the supply chain.

On long-term capacity planning, Ajinomoto recently announced a ¥1.2 billion (approximately $7.6 million) investment to acquire land in Gifu Prefecture, Japan, for the construction of a third ABF plant to meet post-2030 film demand. The plant is scheduled to break ground in 2028, with completion and mass production targeted for 2032. Ajinomoto stated that the Gifu facility will be significantly larger in scale than its existing production bases in Kanagawa and Gunma prefectures.

The company also expects that as AI chip packaging layer counts advance from 3+3 to 11+11, and further toward 13+13 configurations beyond 2030, ABF demand will continue to grow. This technology evolution implies that the ABF film consumption per AI chip will rise exponentially, providing a solid demand foundation for Ajinomoto's long-term growth.

AI Demand Drives Substrates into a New Shortage Cycle

Industry sources indicate that the upgrade cycle for AI CPUs, GPUs, and ASICs is driving both substrate area requirements and layer counts higher simultaneously, with ABF substrates re-entering a shortage state since the first half of 2026. Among Taiwan-based IC substrate manufacturers, Unimicron and Kinsus Interconnect Technology are considered the most aggressive in expanding high-end ABF capacity. Both companies have already announced upward revisions to their 2026 capital expenditure plans to meet strong demand from major AI chip clients such as Nvidia (NVDA) and Google (GOOGL) .

From a broader perspective, the industry widely anticipates a "super-expansion cycle" over the next two to three years, with IC substrate sector order visibility expected to stabilize and continue improving, providing relatively firm support for manufacturers' performance. However, the comprehensive rise in upstream material prices will also test substrate makers' ability to pass through costs and exercise bargaining power.

Market analysts note that the core driver behind this wave of price increases is the rapid iteration of AI chip packaging technology. As advanced packaging evolves from 3+3 to 11+11 and toward 13+13 layers, the ABF film area required per chip increases substantially, while Ajinomoto remains the only global supplier capable of consistently delivering high-quality ABF film. This structural supply-demand imbalance grants Ajinomoto significant pricing leverage while placing sustained cost pressure on downstream substrate manufacturers.

For end customers such as Nvidia and Google, ABF substrate cost increases will ultimately pass through the packaging segment to the chip level. However, amid the current explosion in AI computing demand, supply chain stability and capacity assurance are far more urgent priorities than cost control. This perhaps explains why Ajinomoto can raise prices twice within a single year, with downstream players still regarding the moves as "within a reasonable range."

Looking ahead, with the Gifu plant set to begin production in 2032, Ajinomoto's global supply capacity will further expand. Until then, however, the tight balance in ABF film supply and demand is unlikely to fundamentally change. For substrate makers like Unimicron and Kinsus that are actively expanding capacity, whoever can lock in upstream material supply earlier and more effectively pass through costs downstream will be best positioned to compete in this super-cycle.
