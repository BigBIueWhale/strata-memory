Samsung Foundry Is Said to Increase 4- and 5-nm Prices by Around 15 Percent

11. July 2026 06:00
Karsten Rabeneck-Ketme
📖 Reading time: approx. 5 minutes · 894 words · 5,665 characters
1.0×

No matter how you look at it: anyone having chips manufactured is currently getting very few good news items. After reports of higher TSMC prices, Samsung Foundry is now also said to be raising conditions for advanced manufacturing processes. New customers for 4 nm and 5 nm are said to be particularly affected. This is not a confirmed Samsung price list, but the direction fits quite well into a market environment in which AI demand, limited capacity, and expensive expansion plans have significantly changed the negotiating position of foundries. The starting point is a report by Chosun Biz dated July 9, 2026. According to the report, Samsung Electronics is said to have increased delivery prices for new customers on highly demanded 4- and 5-nm processes by around 15 percent. Selected 8-nm nodes for automotive applications are also said to be affected. Wccftech, SamMobile, and other Samsung-adjacent sites have picked up the report. Samsung itself has not yet officially confirmed the specific price increases.

The report is particularly interesting because Samsung Foundry has been under pressure in recent years. TSMC dominated the leading manufacturing nodes, while Samsung struggled with yield issues, customer migration, and fluctuating utilization. Reports of aggressive pricing offers for 2 nm therefore fit the picture of a provider trying to regain market share. Now the situation appears different. According to Chosun Biz, the pricing logic in the foundry market has shifted: it is no longer weak demand that forces providers to offer discounts, but limited capacity that allows higher prices. AI and HPC customers in particular are changing the calculation. Anyone needing advanced process nodes is competing not only with smartphone SoCs, but also with data center chips, network ASICs, AI accelerators, and automotive applications.

4 nm and 5 nm are long no longer the absolute cutting edge, but that is precisely what makes them economically interesting. While 2 nm and 3 nm are reserved for the latest high-end designs, 4- and 5-nm nodes remain attractive for many chips that need high performance but do not necessarily require the most expensive process. These include smartphone SoCs, network chips, AI-related accelerators, automotive components, and custom designs. If Samsung can enforce higher prices here, it would indicate better utilization and stronger bargaining power. At the same time, it shows that cost pressure is not limited to TSMC. Anyone hoping that Samsung would permanently undercut TSMC as a price-aggressive alternative is now being shown a much more sober market reality.

The report also comes in the shadow of the recent TSMC announcements. TSMC is said to be increasing prices for several advanced nodes, including 3 nm, 5 nm, and 7 nm. According to Chosun Biz, Samsung is not following exactly the same broad line, but appears to be focusing on 4 nm and 5 nm for new customers as well as selected 8-nm automotive processes. That is an important difference. Samsung may be trying to extract margin precisely where demand is currently stable enough and where customers are looking for an alternative to TSMC. The fact that Samsung Foundry is said to have recently been operating profitably on a monthly basis fits this interpretation. However, it would be premature to derive a real turnaround against TSMC from this. Price increases are not proof of technological superiority, but first and foremost a sign that sufficient demand is present.

For end customers, a foundry price increase does not automatically mean that every smartphone, every notebook, or every graphics card will immediately become more expensive. Between wafer price and retail price lie design costs, packaging, memory, distribution, margin, and product strategy. Nevertheless, the direction is important. If wafer prices rise, calculations become tighter. Manufacturers can absorb the costs, save elsewhere, or pass them on gradually. Especially with AI hardware and server components, the market is likely to absorb higher manufacturing costs more readily than in the price-sensitive consumer segment. For classic PC hardware, the effect is therefore more indirect, but not irrelevant. Modern platforms are increasingly tied to the same manufacturing capacities that AI and automotive customers also demand.

Conclusion

I would not present this report as a dramatic Samsung pivot, but it is a good signal for the state of the foundry market. If even Samsung can charge around 15 percent more for 4 nm and 5 nm, then the era of pure discount hunting is apparently over, at least in part. For customers, that is inconvenient; for Samsung, it would be a much-needed margin lever. And for us as buyers, it unfortunately means once again: the next hardware generation will not be determined only by performance, but by the cost of each individual wafer layer.
