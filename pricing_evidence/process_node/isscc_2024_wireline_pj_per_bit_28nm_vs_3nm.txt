What is ISSCC?
ISSCC (International Solid-State Circuits Conference) is the flagship conference of the IEEE Solid-State Circuits Society. According to
the SIA, the Semiconductor industry generated US$574.1 billion in sales in 2022, the highest-ever annual total and an increase of 3.3%
compared to the 2021 total of $555.9 billion, and ISSCC continues to be the premier technical forum for presenting advances in solidstate circuits and systems. According to the World Semiconductor Trade Statistics (WSTS) Fall 2022 Semiconductor Industry Forecast,
worldwide semiconductor industry sales are forecasted to decrease to $556 billion in 2023 and increase to $602 billion in 2024.
Semiconductors are crucial components of electronic devices and the industry is highly competitive. As of September 2023, the monthto-month sales has increased by 1.9%, however, year-to-year sales has decreased by 4.5%).

1.1 Semiconductor Industry: Present & Future

Kevin Zhang, Senior Vice President, Business Development & Overseas Operations Office,
Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company

Semiconductors are the foundation of today’s digital economy and powering innovations that will shape the trajectory of human history.
This paper highlights the latest progress of the semiconductor industry to support a vast spectrum of applications that have forever
changed our lives. It gives insight into the paths of continued advanced technology scaling, the essential role of design-technology cooptimization (DTCO), and how system-level integration will elevate system performance to new heights. The advancements of
semiconductors will enable many new innovations in artificial intelligence (AI), high-performance computing (HPC), wireless connectivity,
and autonomous driving. The paper also provides the trends of technologies ranging from low-power and edge AI devices to cloud-based
computing. By harnessing the new capabilities of semiconductors, these innovations will greatly improve productivity, efficiency, safety,
as well as sustainability. The semiconductor industry is indeed experiencing a “golden era” in spurring remarkable economic growth and
unleash innovations to create a better future for society.

1.2

Racing Down the Slopes of Moore’s Law
Bram Nauta, Professor & Head IC Design Group, University of Twente

During the past decades, Moore’s law has been the driving force for IC design. Although during the early decades, “everything” seemed
to have gotten better, in the meantime, we have lost scaling of processor clock speed and RF transistor speed, and now it looks like
power efficiency of digital gates will stall. What remains is scaling in transistor count, thanks to 3D integration.
Thus, this is an excellent moment to reconsider how we design for analog- and digital-signal processing. The higher the required signalto-noise, the more power-efficient digital signal processing is compared to analog. Pure analog processing remains more efficient only
for ~30dB SNR or less. Thus, the conversion from analog-to-digital should be made as early in the chain as possible. Thanks to the
figure-of-merit race, analog-to-digital converters have experienced a tremendous win in power efficiency. However, these ADCs require
a large input voltage swing while the input signals to be converted, such as from an antenna or sensor interface, are usually much smaller.
Therefore, RF and analog frontends are needed, which consume much more power than the ADCs to be driven.
Let us re-think these analog frontends. Can we still efficiently design these frontends in future CMOS? Do we need so much linear
amplification? Do we need active linear circuits at all? Can we not use “digital” components to replace analog frontends and AD
converters?

1.3

Computing in the Era of Generative AI
Jonah Alben, Senior Vice President, GPU Engineering, NVIDIA

Generative AI has captured the imagination of users across multiple industries, and we have only begun to tap the potential of this
amazing technology. GenAI applications can create text, computer code, protein sequences, images, video, rendered 3D graphics, music,
with more generation types being constantly added. The combination of high compute demands, and the real-time requirements of many
GenAI-based applications requires design thinking at data center scale. This paper will cover the breadth of technology innovations, from
circuits to silicon to software to datacenter, needed to enable today’s latest supercomputers for GenAI, and discuss our experiences
applying GenAI to various business sectors.

1.4

Fueling Semiconductor Innovation and Entrepreneurship
in the Next Decade
Lip-Bu Tan, Chairman of Walden International, Founding Managing partner of Walden Catalyst Ventures,
Senior Advisor & former CEO Cadence Design

This paper provides a comprehensive overview of the future of semiconductor technology, focusing on the interplay between innovation
and entrepreneurship. It is organized into sections that discuss the current state of the semiconductor industry, the role of
entrepreneurship in driving innovation, and the potential future developments in this field. It highlights the virtuous cycle of innovation and
entrepreneurship, where advancements in semiconductor technology fuel new business opportunities, which in turn drive further
technological progress. This cycle is crucial for the creation of the next generation of intelligent electronic systems. The paper also
explores the 'stack' of semiconductor technology, from materials and design to manufacturing and generative AI applications. It
establishes that there are beneficial relationships between these different parts of the stack, with advancements in one area often enabling
progress in others. This interconnectedness underscores the importance of a holistic approach to innovation in the semiconductor
industry, where advancements in different parts of the stack can collectively drive the industry forward.

EE3: Mixed-Foundry Chiplets? Opportunity and Challenge
One of the advantages of chiplets is the ability to integrate chiplets developed from different manufacturing processes to realize SoCs
with optimal performance/$. An I/O chip, for instance, could be built by one foundry, the core processor could be built by another foundry,
and then those chips could be put together on a package. With this, there’s potential for mixing and matching chiplets from different
foundries. To make this happen, many challenges need to be overcome for the industry, including standardized interfaces between
chiplets, verification of whole SoCs using chiplets made from different processes, design flows from multiple foundries, and reliability
assurance, including thermal and electromagnetic interactions between chiplets. A mixed-foundry chiplet ecosystem will be crucial to
facilitate productization of complex systems-on-chiplet.

Tuesday, February 21
EE4: Generative AI for Chip Design
With the emergence of machine learning and generative AI, many types of jobs are being transformed by GPT-based tools. Large
Language Models are starting to be used for education and can be used to contribute to publications, and AI is being embedded into
EDA tools. Join this evening panel with experts from industry and academia to discuss how generative AI or AI in general will change IC
design.

Tuesday, February 21
EE5: The Legacy of Gordon Moore
Moore’s Law has propelled the semiconductor industry for decades, transforming the world through advancements in digital electronics,
and to some extent, analog and RF electronics. These advancements have fueled other engineering fields such as artificial intelligence,
biomedical engineering and quantum engineering. In this session, we will have a fireside chat with semiconductor and IC design
luminaries celebrating the life and legacy of Gordon Moore, discussing the impact of Moore’s law on our industry, and venturing into the
next chapter of Moore’s law in the context of upcoming IC ecosystems.

Session 2 Overview: Processors & Communication SoCs
Digital Architectures and Systems Subcommittee
Session Chair: Shidhartha Das, AMD, UK
Session Co-Chair: Sugako Otani, Renesas Electronics, Japan
Mobile and general-purpose processors continue to push the envelope of performance and power efficiency up to and beyond 5nm. The
first three papers show a paradigm shift for mobile computing, followed by generational upgrades in mainstream compute. Latencycritical AI and ray tracing are applied to edge applications in the fourth and fifth papers. The last three papers feature emerging hardware
for advanced data communications.
●

In Paper 2.1, MediaTek describes the CPU cluster from its latest 5G smartphone SoC: the first published mobile SoC to feature
out-of-order cores in all CPU gears. The 3.4GHz CPUs, built in 4nm, integrate a novel first-level cache design to deliver a 4.6%
CPU power reduction, while other power reduction techniques combine to realize a 22% system-level power benefit.

●

In Paper 2.2, AMD details a same-node area-optimization of its 5nm Zen4 CPU core. Targeting processor density in a 73mm2
die, the optimized core features a 35% smaller L2 cache and 25% smaller CPU core for >25% improvement in performance/mm2
and 9% improvement in performance/W, enabling a 33% increase in cores per package.

●

In Paper 2.3, Intel presents Emerald Rapids, the 5th-generation Xeon® Scalable Processors built on the Intel 7 process node.
Featuring up to 64 cores and over 300MB of shared L3 cache, the multichip package delivers iso-power performance uplift of
18%/24% for integer/floating-point workloads, respectively, as compared to the prior generation.

●

In Paper 2.4, Rebellions describes their SoC for latency-critical AI inference tasks that require prompt response. Supporting up
to 32TFLOPS (FP16) and 128TOPS (INT8), the 5nm SoC delivers a 0.239ms latency for ResNet50 single stream.

●

In Paper 2.5, Northwestern University presents a 28nm 3.56mm2 physical-based ray-tracing rendering processor for
photorealistic augmented reality. Their work enables photo-realistic real-time physical-based ray tracing on mobile devices by
embedding end-to-end inverse rendering that achieves 3.95-to-28.8´ higher render efficiency than existing ASICs.

●

In Paper 2.6, National Taiwan University demonstrates a multi-user MIMO OTFS detector, featuring Doppler spread resilience,
for next-generation communications systems. The 40nm 9.73mm2 chip delivers a maximum throughput of 6.4Gbps,
representing a 3.3-to-18.3 increase over state-of-the-art OFDM detectors, while also realizing a 2.2-to-30.1´ improvement in
normalized energy.

●

In Paper 2.7, Southeast University details BayesBB, a 19mm2 configurable all-message-passing baseband accelerator for
beyond-5G/6G cell-free massive MIMO in 40nm. Achieving a throughput of 9.6Gbps, a latency of 1.61ms, and integrating 10
Gbps SERDES links, the SoC demonstrates >100bps/Hz when tested with a 128´128 MIMO array.

●

In Paper 2.8, Pohang University of Science and Technology demonstrates the integration of a BOSS decoder as a nextgeneration FEC for 5G/6G applications. Offering the best solution among recent URLLC FEC designs, this work achieves a
latency of 21.9ns and an area efficiency of 15.7Gbps/mm2 in a 28nm technology.

CONTEXT AND STATE OF THE ART
●

Emerging wireless, wireline, and sensing applications often demand ADCs with unprecedented bandwidths, linearities, and
spectral efficiencies, often extending into the 10s and even 100s of gigahertz.

●

Many techniques, some new, some old, are being explored and utilized to enable higher bandwidths: continuous-time pipeline,
time-to-digital converter, high-speed channels, massive interleaving, efficient input networks, and background calibrations.

●

High-speed Nyquist ADCs typically must combine many time-interleaved sub-ADCs (channels) in parallel to achieve the target
speeds. The input buffer driving these sub-ADCs, and the front-end routing network connecting them together are often key
performance bottlenecks. Robust and efficient background calibration techniques are essential for detecting and cancelling
timing skew and bandwidth-related errors in the front-end.

●

The continuous-time pipelined ADC (CT-pipeline) offers a promising alternative to pure Nyquist ADCs. It is well suited for
applications demanding low noise and high linearity. Despite their typically lower bandwidths and relatively high power
consumption, the inherent anti-aliasing property of a CT-Pipeline, enabled by its over-sampled, noise-shaping principle of
operation, and continuous-time nature, relaxes upstream signal chain requirements, offering strong incentive at the system
level for applications such as direct-RF conversion.

●

The feasibility, and cost-competitiveness of both wireless 5G and 6G, and wireline serial links serving applications such as

At very high speeds, utilization of background calibration techniques and digitally-assisted analog are, to varying degrees,

Session 34 Overview: Compute In Memory
Memory Subcommittee
Session Chair: Eric Wang, TSMC, Hsinchu, Taiwan
Session Co-Chair: Ankur Agrawal, IBM Research, Yorktown Heights, USA
Memory access is the major bottleneck for system performance and energy consumption in traditional von Neumann system
architectures. A compute-in-memory (CIM) architecture eliminates this bottleneck by bringing compute operations into the memory array.
Innovations in CIM design continue to improve energy and area efficiencies while maintaining overall AI network accuracy. This session
showcases the latest developments in SRAM and non-volatile CIM: showcasing increased energy efficiency, throughput, precision, and
accuracy. Featured innovations include a dual-mode (integer/floating-point) gain-cell, a ReRAM-based non-volatile CIM that performs
floating-point compute, and the first foundry 6T SRAM-DCIM in 3nm technology.
●

In Paper 34.1, Tsinghua University describes the first digital CIM macro supporting POSIT number representation formats. It
achieves 83.23TFLOPS/W in a 28nm process.

●

In Paper 34.2, TSMC presents a CIM macro employing a dual-mode gain-cell capable of performing both integer and floatingpoint multiply-and-accumulate operations. The 16-nm 96-kb macro achieves 91.2TFLOPS/W for BFLOAT16 computations.

●

In Paper 34.3, Southeast University demonstrates a hybrid analog-digital SRAM CIM macro, with a novel division of compute
between analog and digital domains. It reports the highest energy efficiency (60.8TOPS/W) for INT8 operations with no loss of
precision in 22nm CMOS.

●

In Paper 34.4, TSMC introduces a 3nm SRAM-based digital CIM macro that is based on the foundry’s 6T SRAM bit-cell arrays
and a parallel MAC scheme to improve the bit-cell density and operation throughput. The DCIM achieves 55.0TOPS/mm2 with
a bit-cell density of 3.78Mb/mm2.

●

In Paper 34.5, Keio University presents an accuracy-reconfigurable analog CIM macro for CNNs and transformers. It achieves
4094TOPS/W in 1b CNN mode, and a 45dB SQNR and a 31dB CSNR in transformer mode.

●

In Paper 34.6, IME-CAS demonstrates a hybrid-domain (analog/digital) floating-point SRAM CIM macro. The 28nm chip shows
16.55 - 72.12TFLOPS/W in BFloat16 computations.

●

In Paper 34.7, Tsinghua University showcases an eDRAM-LUT-based DCIM macro. The macro features in-memory encoding
and refresh; it achieves an energy efficiency of 18.1TOPS/W, with an area density of 2.4Mb/mm2 in a 28nm technology.

●

In Paper 34.8, NTHU describes a 16-Mb ReRAM-based non-volatile CIM macro in 22nm CMOS, capable of computing in both
FP16 and BFloat16 formats. It achieves an energy efficiency of 28.7 - 31.2TFLOPS/W.

●

In Paper 34.9, IME-CAS presents a logic-Flash/SRAM hybrid CIM macro in 14nm CMOS. It supports long- and short-term
plasticity-based learning and achieves an energy efficiency of 1449TOPS/W, normalized to 1b MACs.

Power Management – 2024 Trends
Subcommittee Chair: Bernhard Wicht, Leibniz University, Hannover, Germany
Power management encompasses a diverse set of applications ranging from automotive, microprocessor compute, 5G/6G
communications, energy harvesting, wireless power transfer and various other consumer and industrial applications. The diverse
applications possess a variety of requirements, including sub-1V voltages needed for next generation computing systems, often
generated from very high voltages (48V). Some applications such as 5G/6G have supply modulators requiring power-management
solutions to respond to near-instantaneous changes in voltage and current levels, while other applications like automotive require extreme
reliability and careful design to minimize electromagnetic interference (EMI). Some must transfer power and/or signals across isolation
boundaries, and yet others must efficiently process and recover energy from intermittent low-power transducers. Thus, while there is no
one-size solution that fits all power-management applications, common key performance indicators (KPIs) for power- management
techniques are to achieve high performance, minimal power loss, small size, and low cost.
Trends seen at this year’s ISSCC continue to address the growing diversity of applications and challenges outlined above. Power
conversion topologies continue to evolve, many leveraging hybrid architectures that mix switched-capacitor (SC) designs with inductor(s).
These hybrid architectures, provide seamless voltage mode transitions across a wide range of input-output voltages, and offer high
efficiency and reduced inductor sizes to improve performance metrics across the board. A number of these topologies focus on exploiting
additional low-voltage switches and capacitors to reduce the inductor sizes which often are the biggest bottleneck to improving density.
Additionally, there are a variety of efforts to address key challenges in hybrid designs such as balancing flying capacitors and achieving
safe startup, and also efforts to improve voltage regulation and transient response.
This year we also see growth in wireless power transfer (WPT) applied to various fields, including smartphones, medical devices, smart
homes, and electric vehicles. Increasing the transmission power of the WPT system can improve the charging speed of devices, which
is particularly important in portable electronic devices while improving system efficiency and load regulation are crucial metrics for
evaluating a WPT system. Hybrid Class-ED power-amplifier (PA) architectures, that combine the high output power of Class-E, and the
low voltage stress of Class-D are presented to boost the charging speeds. Meanwhile mode-recognition techniques to completely
deactivate the PA and phase-shift/time-constant detection techniques for effective mode detection under a wide range of coupling
coefficients are efforts to substantially improve efficiency of the WPT system.
We continue to see a good majority of innovations on integrated voltage regulators (IVRs) demonstrating higher current/power densities,
with higher efficiency and faster transient response for next generation computing needs. Innovative topologies interfacing high input
voltages and large conversion ratios that enhance efficiency with smaller inductors, fully monolithic SC topology innovations that facilitate
achieving >10W/mm2 densities, and high-current chiplet-based regulators with package embedded magnetic inductors are some efforts
to showcase notable achievements in achieving high power density. Even traditionally stable areas such as analog LDOs have
innovations to improve scalability in current to cater to a wider range of use cases.
Other areas of focus at ISSCC 2024 include monolithic GaN ICs, envelope tracking, isolated and/or offline converters, high voltage EV
chargers and energy harvesting. Fully integrated 500V GaN ICs switching at 500MHz for fast slew-rate high-efficiency PFC applications,
and supply modulators with 200MHz envelope-tracking bandwidth are presented. In automotive applications, topologies for fast EV
charging, control techniques for LiDAR, and techniques tailored for better EMI suppression to comply with CISPR25 standards are also
presented. Finally, isolated DC-DC converters with full-duplex communication, SC-based AC-DC solutions with better light-load efficiency
and continuously scalable-conversion-ratio SC-based energy-harvesting power-converter techniques round off the innovations at this
year’s ISSCC in power management.

Data Converters – 2024 Trends
Subcommittee Chair: Jan Westra, Broadcom, Bunnik, The Netherlands
Data converters play an important role in wireline, wireless applications, and sensor interfaces. The major challenge in data converters
has been energy efficiency while delivering the required bandwidth and signal fidelity. This year at ISSCC, we will see the continuing
effort of pushing the boundaries of energy efficiency along with thinking beyond the conventional methods and introduction of new
architectures and techniques.
Being successful in delivering wide bandwidth and high resolution, hybrid pipelined ADC designs are using different techniques to make
the mid-stage amplifiers stable with PVT, accurate, and low power. High-speed and time-interleaving architectures are leaning more
toward time-based quantization and more efficient mismatch tuning techniques.
The three figures below represent traditional metrics that capture the innovative progress in ADC design. The first figure plots power
dissipated relative to the Nyquist sampling rate (P/fsnyq), as a function of signal-to-noise and distortion ratio (SNDR), to give a measure of
ADC power efficiency. Note that a lower P/fsnyq metric represents a more efficient circuit on this chart. For low-to-medium-resolution
converters, energy is primarily expended to quantize the signal; thus the overall efficiency of this operation is typically measured by the
energy consumed per conversion and quantization step. The dashed trend-line represents a benchmark of 1fJ/conversion-step. Circuit
noise becomes more significant with higher-resolution converters, necessitating a different benchmark proportional to the square of the
signal-to-noise ratio, represented by the solid line. Designs published from 1997 to 2022 are shown in circles. ISSCC 2024 designs are
shown in red stars.
The second figure plots signal fidelity vs. the Nyquist sampling rate normalized to power consumption. At low sampling rates, converters
tend to be limited by thermal and flicker noises, independent of the sample rate. Higher speeds of operation present additional challenges
in maintaining accuracy in an energy-efficient manner, indicated by the roll-off vs. frequency in the dashed line. The last ten years have
resulted in an improvement of over 10dB in power-normalized signal fidelity, or a 10× improvement in speed for the same normalized
signal fidelity. At ISSCC this year, hybrid pipelined-SAR and noise-shaping SARs are continuing the trends in the speed vs efficiency
corner of the graph. Time-based conversion of signal and time-domain quantization are becoming an integral part of converters in scaled
nodes. Time-interleaving multiple channels with error correction over process, voltage and temperature (PVT) as well as clock skew
between channels is a necessity in these architectures to achieve robust performance in high-speed conversion rates.
The final figure plots ADC bandwidth as a function of SNDR. Sampling jitter or aperture errors deteriorate the ADC resolution and signal
bandwidth. In recent years we have seen designs with aperture errors below 100fsrms published at ISSCC.

RF – 2024 Trends
Subcommittee Chair: Brian Ginsburg, Texas Instruments, TX
The RF papers to be presented at ISSCC 2024 demonstrate record-setting advancements in phase-locked loops (PLLs), voltagecontrolled oscillators (VCOs) and multipliers, RF power amplifiers (PAs), and signal-generation/detection techniques. These innovations
are driven by emerging requirements in 5G and 6G communications, the Internet of Things (IoT), radars, and imaging at RF, mm-wave,
and THz frequencies. This document provides a highlight of these trends.
Frequency Synthesis:
At ISSCC 2024, papers will be presented introducing new PLL concepts for generating RF, microwave, and mm-wave frequency carriers.
These papers feature concept demonstrations with record-setting achievements in low-jitter, low-power-consumption, and simplified
calibration. These achievements extend the number of publications with jitter-power FoM below -250dB for both fractional-N and integerN PLLs, as shown in Fig. 1.
For example, an 8.75GHz fractional-N digital PLL employs a reverse concave variable-slope DTC with digitally calibrated analog
predistortion to attain 57.3fsrms jitter, a -63.4dBc fractional spur, and a -252.4dB FoM. Additionally, an 8GHz fractional-N digital PLL
implements a recursive-least-squares algorithm with low hardware complexity to shorten the convergence time to 5.5µs and achieve a
fractional spur of -68dBc, jitter of 88fsrms, and -249.1dB FoM. A 7GHz fractional-N digital PLL shifts the near-integer fractional spurs
outside the PLL bandwidth using two delta-sigma modulators to control a multimodulus divider and DTCs. This PLL delivers DPD-free
worst-case fractional spur of -62.1dBc, 143.7fsrms jitter, and -247.8dB FoM. Another 6.56GHz fractional-N PLL uses an enhanced
nonlinearity-induced noise performance (ENOP) digital delta-sigma modules to suppress spurs and achieve a 45.5fsrms random jitter, 75dBc worst-case fractional spur, and -118dBc reference spur. Lastly, an 11.1GHz fractional-N digital sampling PLL utilizes a replica
nonlinearity DAC to cancel the nonlinearity of the sampling phase detector and achieves -65dBc fractional spur, 76fsrms jitter. and 250.2dB FoM.
Furthermore, two papers present FMCW modulators. One, based on a digital PLL, achieves a chirp slope of 682MHz/µs with a 0.02%
rms frequency error over a 680MHz chirp bandwidth at 9.25-to-10.5GHz output frequency. It exhibits 87.1fsrms jitter and an FoM of 248dB. The other, based on an analog PLL, achieves a chirp slope of 2.3GHz/µs with an rms frequency error of 0.051% over a 2.3GHz
chirp bandwidth at 9.6-to-12.4GHz output frequency. It utilizes a 2nd-order DPD to compensate for the DCO nonlinearity.
In addition, two papers present integer-N PLLs. One demonstrates a 281GHz frequency generator with -1.5dBm output power, 46 fsrms
jitter integrated over 1kHz to 10MHz, and 86fsrms jitter integrated over 1kHz to 100MHz. This is achieve using cascaded subharmonicallyinjection-locked sub-sampling PLLs and an H-band frequency doubler. The second paper demonstrates a sub-sampling PLL achieving
a -66dBc reference spur, 48.3fsrms jitter, and -253.5dB FoM at 23.2-to-26GHz output frequencies.
These integer-N and fractional-N PLLs continue to improve power consumption and integrated jitter to keep pace with performance
targets in communications and sensing applications.

High-performance electrical and optical interconnects stand as pivotal catalysts for bandwidth-hungry electronic devices, computing
servers, and telecommunication networks. Driven by the soaring demands of AI, the exponential growth in system scaling relies on
greener, denser, and more reliable data links. These encompass a spectrum of applications, including 3D chip fabric, chiplet and copackage integration, displays, backplanes, rack-to-rack connections, and LAN setups. As per-lane data rates surge beyond 100Gb/s,
wireline links confront a host of challenges, from energy efficiency to ISI resilience and cross-talk immunity, all critical to sustaining
ever-increasing system performance. This progress owes itself to innovations in equalization, signaling, clocking techniques, and
the enhanced power-performance characteristics of advanced technology nodes. Figure 1 illustrates that data rates per channel
have approximately doubled every four years across a range of I/O standards, spanning DDR, storage, and high-speed Ethernet.
Figure 2 shows that the data rates for published transceivers are pushed beyond 100Gb/s while taking advantage of technology
scaling. Figure 3 shows published transceiver energy efficiency vs. channel loss catering to applications spanning from short reach
to long distance applications.

Pushing Electrical Interconnects to 200Gb/s and Beyond
With the exponential growth of artificial intelligence systems and cloud computing, the next generation wireline transceivers are
aiming for data rates of 800GbE/1.6TbE, requiring 224Gb/s per lane. High-speed wireline communication requires high-bandwidth
ADCs and DACs with low jitter PLLs. In Paper 7.1, Marvell presents a complete DSP-based PAM-4 transceiver at 212Gb/s for
optical direct detect application, with an analog power efficiency of 2.69pJ/b in 5nm FinFET. In Paper 7.2, Intel presents a 224Gb/s
transmitter based on a 7b DAC driver with 9-tap FFE, fabricated in 3nm FinFET technology. The TX uses quarter-rate 28GHz clocking
for a combined single-stage, current-mode 4:1 MUX and driver with a replica driver for phase adaptation. An LC-PLL with a tuning
range from 21.2 to 30.0GHz allows operation at both PAM-4 and PAM-6 modes. The TX achieves 1Vppd swing and 0.92pJ/b analog
energy efficiency while showing 36.0dB SNDR, 55mUI and 62fsrms jitter. In Paper 7.3, Synopsys presents a long-reach capable,
224Gb/s PAM-4 wireline transceiver solution achieving sub 1e-6 BER with 35dB insertion loss, combined with an analog energy
efficiency of 3pJ/b. The design comprises a 7b DAC-based transmitter and an inverter-based RX front-end with 7b ADC. The
transceiver benefits from a digitally calibrated 1/8-rate clocking system, achieving 55fsrms random and 170fs deterministic jitter.

Optical Links for Long-Haul to Short Reach Interconnects
With growing demand for higher bandwidth in network and telecommunication infrastructure, coherent optical transceivers are
pushing the speed limits in the already popular long-haul (LH) and ultra-long-haul (ULH) interface applications and expanding their
usage to data center Ethernet switching and artificial intelligence (AI) applications. At the same time, co-packaged optics (CPO)

The papers in this session describe transmitter and receiver architectures that enable high-speed yet low-power operation. In Paper
18.1, Broadcom presents a 600Gb/s DP-QAM-64 coherent optical transceiver frontend which incorporates 4-lane configuration of
105GS/s 8b ADCs and DACs in the receiver and transmitter respectively. Designed in 16nm CMOS technology, the ADC-based
receiver achieves a -3dB bandwidth of 40GHz, SNDR/SFDR of 39.2dB/50.7dB at 20.9GHz input frequency. The integrated jitter of
the transceiver PLL was measured at 51fs. In Paper 18.3, Marvell describes an 8b 160GS/s optical transmitter fully integrated in an

PAM-4 data rate of 279.2Gb/s, and an energy efficiency of less than 0.9pJ/b with the assistance of a proposed time-interleaved DAC
calibration technique. In Paper 18.4, Marvell demonstrates an 8b 200GS/s wireline receiver for coherent optical application, designed
in a 5nm FinFET process. Exploiting several bandwidth extension techniques such as negatively coupled T-coil and active inductor

speed of 200GS/s, and a Walden FoM of 20 fJ/c-s at a 0.3GHz input frequency.
Low Jitter Clock Generator for High Performance Wireline Transceiver
A low-jitter multi-phase clock generator is pivotal in high-speed serial link transceivers. In Paper 7.4, the University of Macau presents
a ping-pong sampling phase detector that extends the loop bandwidth of the ring PLL and improves the phase margin. An edge
correction loop is devised to align the sampling edges. Operating at 6.6GHz, the proposed PLL, fabricated in 65nm CMOS, scores
a 220.3fsrms integrated jitter, −74.2dBc reference spur and −241dB jitter-power figure of-merit. The frequency tuning range is 33.3%,
and the active area is 0.027mm2. In Paper 7.8, the University of Illinois presents a type-III ring-based eight-phase sampling PLL
aided by an additional integrator and an NMOS-based regulator. The PLL, fabricated in a 22nm FinFET process, achieves a
continuous tuning range from 6.8GHz to 14GHz, 69.3fs jitter at 13GHz and 31.4dB improvement in spur level suppression under
50mVpp supply noise.

Digital Circuits – 2024 Trends
Subcommittee Chair: Keith Bowman, Qualcomm, Raleigh, NC
The enduring demand for flexible and energy-efficient platforms, ranging from low-power sensors to large inference engines and generalpurpose processors, continues to drive innovations in CMOS digital circuits and accelerators. Digital circuit innovations continue to
leverage emerging technologies, such as nonvolatile memories and advanced metallization, and increasingly adopt run-time inference
to augment traditional circuit capabilities. Meanwhile, reliability and power-delivery challenges that arise from implementations using
advanced process technology, or operation in challenging environments (e.g., automotive) continue to drive circuit and system innovation.
New circuit architectures for domain-specific accelerators continue to be actively explored over a range of emerging applications that
include optimization problems (e.g., Ising or SAT-solvers) and artificial intelligence at video and audio processing. A notable trend in
optimization solvers has been an emphasis on increasing the scale and complexity of problems that can be mapped and solved on chip.
Meanwhile, accelerators for edge applications are witnessing the increased adoption of compute-in-memory (CIM) architectures. Both
digital CIM and mixed-signal architectures employing nonvolatility are being actively explored with the objective of optimizing energy
efficiency. In addition, improvements to traditional digital circuit blocks for integrated power management and clocking continue to be
pursued.
Integrated Voltage Regulators (VRs) and Power-Delivery Designs: The continued push for power-constrained performance in scaled
technologies has exacerbated challenges in power delivery and point-of-load (POL) voltage regulation. Supply noise and VR transient
response remain key sources of system inefficiency. Meanwhile, 3D integration places additional constraints on power distribution
network (PDN) and decoupling capacitance resources. Addressing these obstacles in an increasingly challenging landscape has led
digital designers to implement processors that are better informed by PDN and VR constraints, both at design and run time. This year’s
ISSCC papers describe processors that modulate their current draw based on VR current limitations and supply noise. VR designs
continue to scale peak current density capabilities for effective POL power delivery. Figure 1 illustrates the current density achieved by
integrated VRs in recent years, with an order-of-magnitude increase reported this year. Another notable trend in VR design includes a
growing interest in circuit architectures for distributed VR control and a steady growth in achievable peak VR current density to enable
area-efficient, high-performance multi-domain systems.

COMPUTE IN MEMORY
Memory access is a major bottleneck to system performance and energy consumption in traditional von Neumann system
architectures. A compute-in-memory (CIM) architecture eliminates this bottleneck by bringing compute operations into the memory
array, thereby significantly reducing IO power dissipation. Innovations in CIM designs continue to improve energy and area efficiencies
while maintaining overall AI network accuracy. This session showcases the latest developments in SRAM and non-volatile-memorybased CIM systems: with increased energy efficiency, throughput, precision, and accuracy. Featured innovations include a dual-mode
(integer/floating-point) gain-cell, a ReRAM-based non-volatile CIM that performs floating-point compute, and the first foundry 6T SRAMDCIM in 3nm technology.

Security – 2024 Trends
Subcommittee Chair: Ingrid Verbauwhede, KU Leuven, Leuven, Belgium
The increasingly connected nature of embedded systems and the ever-increasing risk of cyber-attacks,
continues to make hardware security a must-have feature. ISSCC continues to feature the state-of-art
implementations of circuit techniques that form the basis of security guarantees that are required by these
systems. The work presented builds on three themes. Firstly we have cost-effective and robust root-of-trust
circuits such as PUFs (physically unclonable functions) and TRNGs (true random-number generators). These
circuits when integrated into larger SoCs underpin critical operations like secret key generation. Next we continue
to have innovative cryptographic accelerator designs that implement the latest algorithms such as post-quantum
cryptography and homomorphic encryption. These designs provide an early look at the technology required to
keep us safe multiple decades into the future. Finally techniques to counteract sophisticated physical attacks are
being integrated into work presented at the conference providing a glimpse of how hardened designs can achieve
higher levels of security with modest increases in cost and complexity.
More specifically, this year’s selection of security papers highlights a mix of the most advanced security
techniques implemented in hardware. Next-generation cryptographic accelerator designs are presented which
include the world’s first bootstrapping capable ASIC implementation of Fully Homomorphic Encryption as well
as a multi-scheme Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) processor. On the root-of-trust circuits side we have PUF and TRNG
implementations at the most advanced 3-5nm CMOS nodes. The experimental evaluations of these implementations are also
accompanied with theoretical modeling to build more confidence in the underlying security claims. A mix of innovative attack
countermeasures is presented including on-chip monitor for fault injection as well as protection against probing attacks and side-channel
attacks.

IMMD – 2024 Trends (Medical)
Subcommittee Chair: Rikky Muller, University of California, Berkeley, CA
Wearable and implantable biomedical systems continue to advance to sense and interpret a broader range of biomedical signals with
improved energy efficiency and accuracy. These state-of-the-art SoCs accurately capture weak nervous and body signals through various
modalities, such as voltage, current, impedance, light, and acoustics. With enhanced intelligence from more sophisticated algorithms
implemented on an energy-efficient hardware architecture, they form closed-loop recording-decoding-stimulation systems, which are not
only pivotal for medical therapy but also serve as key instruments for scientific research.
This year's ISSCC showcases biomedical circuits and systems that significantly expand the boundaries of current technology. Artificial
neural networks decoding the functions of various brain regions including the medial septum, hippocampus, entorhinal cortex, and visual
cortex are demonstrated with improved complexity and accuracy. The introduction of a hybrid-feature-driven adaptive processing to a
zero-shot-retaining seizure detection processor promises to reduce patient hospitalization time while offering better sensitivity and
specificity. An EEG recording system seamlessly embedded into a VR device facilitates a brain-machine interface for in situ mind imagery
control. A high-channel-count miniaturized brain-machine interface classifies handwritten letters by decoding action potential and local
field potential signals through a novel machine learning architecture. A low-power closed-loop neuromodulation system is implanted in a
freely moving monkey.
Innovative wireless communication and power telemetry techniques are aimed at prolonging system longevity by reducing communication
power overhead and improving the link efficiency. While UWB and near-field inductive data and power-transfer methods continue to
improve their performance, emerging modalities such as magnetoelectric and beam-formed ultrasound telemetry are proving their worth.
Artifact tolerant analog front-end schemes accomplish wider dynamic range, lower noise and smaller energy consumption, thereby
improving the sensing precision and robustness. Innovations in miniaturized and flexible system implementations are expected to ensure
minimally invasive and seamless integration of biomedical electronics into human life.

IMMD – 2024 Trends (Imagers)
Subcommittee Chair: Rikky Muller, University of California, Berkeley, CA
A higher demand for image processing capabilities at lower power levels has prompted novel sensor-level machine learning chip
architectures. The low-light performance of a SPAD sensor is used to describe a spiking neural-network (SNN) that can also be configured
as an image signal processor, achieving 10,000 inferences/s based on 100k raw binary frames per second, and resulting in a 99.2%
classification accuracy on the MNIST database, under 10 lx of light. On a similar note, a convolutional neural network (CNN) guided 3D
Inpainting mechanism is also demonstrated, while introducing a novel, high-gain current-integrating-based SiPM analog front-end to
remove offsets due to background light, as well as a compensation for pulse energy and arrival time errors, achieving 240m range and
less than 0.1% precision, for automotive LiDAR. A novel approach using a 3-layer optical-electronic hybrid CNN is presented, which
relies on optical processing as the first convolution layer to achieve low power consumption, while the other two electronic convolution
layers add flexibility to the classifier. It achieves 96.4% accuracy on the MNIST database at 40-100pJ/frame/pixel energy consumption.
In the continuing quest for lower power consumption, an imager with in-sensor event detection and object segmentation is described
through a tile-level analog circuitry for background subtraction and event detection, avoiding readout in uninteresting regions, achieving
object segmentation with 2.9 pJ/pixel*frame figure of merit. Samsung Semiconductor reports a 50MPixel CMOS Image Sensor, with
lower figure of merit, and the record pixel pitch of 0.5µm, using a contact wall scheme to mitigate coupling problems.
SPAD-based direct time-of-flight integrated sensors continue to push the boundaries of high-quality depth and hardware optimization.
Two papers on chip size and power reduction are presented. The first work proposes the replacement of large and power-hungry digital
timing references by an analog counterpart, achieving 21´ lower power and 47% area reduction, while performing per-pixel
histogramming in a 160´120 array, to achieve up to 24m range and 2.9cm accuracy. The second work presents a pixel fill-factor
enhancement by implementing a simplified, nMOS-only, front-end, while reducing the overall chip size through a 2-step histogram
operation with dynamic memory reuse and allocation. Measurements demonstrate up to 48m range, 11cm accuracy, at a resolution of
320´240.
In the field of ultrasound, this year's ISSCC demonstrates various systems and building blocks, targeting applications from neural
interfaces to air-channel imaging. An impressive 1.21mW power delivery is achieved while providing 12Mb/s of data, through a 4×4
ultrasound multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) uplink, whereas a dual-frequency imaging system for UAV applications is also
demonstrated, achieving 0.5° of spatial resolution, at 21 fps(low freq.)+21 fps(high freq.), and a maximum range of 7m. Advances in
transmitter architectures are also presented, including higher TX efficiency, as well as architecture simplifications and component count
reduction.

IMMD – 2024 Trends (Displays)
Subcommittee Chair: Rikky Muller, University of California, Berkeley, CA
The display driver provides an interface function between a microprocessor, microcontroller and peripheral display devices. These are
used in display devices such as personal computers, smartphones and automotive displays. The display driver ICs are growing due to
the increased use of 4K and 8K televisions and beyond. Since the market is demanding higher-quality images, high resolution and high
refresh rate are strong demands. To address these demands, source-driver ICs are required to have fast slew rate buffers with low cost.
KAIST and LX Semicon reveal a 10b source driver IC, developed to accommodate 240Hz super-frame-rate mobile displays which enables
to shorten the 1-horizontal time to 1.5µs without losing die area efficiency. Korea University and C&Tech describe a compact 10b source
driver IC which provides fully nonlinear gamma voltages while achieving the smallest silicon area of 2411µm² per channel.
The capacitive touch-screen panel system is widely used as a de-facto standard touch user interface for smart phones and tablets.
Generally, touch signals are smaller than external noise such as display noise, fluorescent lamps noise and charger noise. Differential
sensing configurations have been used conventionally to suppress the effects of common noise. However, in recent flexible displays the
capacitance value between the touch detection node and the noise source node has been increasing. These large capacitances make it
more difficult to deal with the external noise. Two touch-screen panel systems will be disclosed to address this issue at ISSCC 2024.
Both papers are able to handle a load capacitance of 390 pF or more, and also address dual (mutual- and self-capacitance) sensing.
The third paper presents an energy-efficient touch sensor that creates a low noise frequency band. It achieves an energy efficiency of
25.2pJ/step, which is more than a 4.3´ improvement over the state of the art.

TD – 2024 Trends
Subcommittee Chair:
There have been several new trends in the technology directions this year. These encompass various areas including sensing,
computations, interface with electromagnetics, and quantum computing interfaces and qubits.
Sensing and computing technologies are advancing and finding significant applications across a wide range of fields, notably in
healthcare, environmental monitoring, as well as various applications of keyword recognition and sensor technology. ISSCC 2024
includes innovative systems for bio-implants including an active echo technique with multi-coils to improve wireless power transmission
and robustness and a wireless implant for fluorescence microscopy. Several sensor technologies hold the promise to advance the field
of biomedical and biosensing including miniature multi-nuclei magnetic resonance systems, droplet microfluidics with embedded CMOS
luminescence and impedance sensors, and ferrofluidic multi-functional biomolecular processing. ISSCC 2024 showcases advancements
in brain treatments including a mini-coil magnetic stimulator that offers less invasive placement within the human skull as well as a noninvasive portable intra-cranial pressure sensor. Additionally, technologies that enable high-accuracy keyword spotting down to the nWlevel power are demonstrated. Finally, in line with the conference theme of ICs for a Better World, ISSCC 2024 demonstrates a newly
developed, biodegradable organic transistor with carbon electrodes and a battery composed only of soil fertilizer elements.
The ways in which integrated circuits interface with the electromagnetic spectrum continue to be explored in new and creative ways.
We see continued improvements in ways that electronics can be used to modulate light with extremely low power consumption. We
also see new and creative ways in which to modulate and process RF information, drawing from both the machine learning literature,
as well as techniques normally associated with photonics. Additionally, new RF and mm-Wave back-scatter techniques continue to be
developed to carry more, and different information, while RF wireless power transfer techniques continue to improve in efficiency and
peak power levels.
Quantum computing has emerged over the past decade as a new computational paradigm with the potential to enable certain classes
of computation that are currently impractical given our existing digital computer technology. However, implementation of a fault-tolerant
quantum computer—widely believed to be essential for harnessing the full power of quantum computing— will require building systems
with several orders of magnitude more qubits than is currently possible. This, in turn, will require developing integrated-circuit-based
control and measurement systems to run a large-scale quantum processor. To ease the challenge of system integration, it is attractive
to cryogenically cool these electronics, so that they can be brought near the quantum processor. Designing these circuits for operation
at deep cryogenic temperatures (i.e., <20K) imposes new challenges on circuit designers, such as tolerating extreme PVT while
reducing power levels towards the limit. ISSCC 2024 reports progress toward this goal, with papers describing SoCs optimized for
control and readout of different qubit technologies. Critical advances include a cryo-CMOS SoC demonstrating the feasibility of
simplified, and, hence, low power, microwave control of fluxonium qubits; a cryogenic single-qubit cryo-CMOS SoC demonstrating
control of NV color-center qubits; a cryo-CMOS readout IC demonstrating readout of double-quantum dots with 99.9% fidelity; and the
demonstration of transmon qubit readout using a cryo-CMOS SoC. In addition to this, an advance in electron paramagnetic resonancebased sensing is reported at the conference, with advances in performance enabled by a new IC that supports both CW (chirped) and
pulsed operation.
Similar to last year, ISSCC 2024 includes a session focused on presenting out-of-the-box ideas for the future, highlighting topics lessfamiliar to ISSCC that push the envelope of solid-state circuits and systems that are bound to inspire the next generation of engineers
and scientists. These sessions cover a wide range of topics including photonic transceivers, metamaterials and metasurfaces for
extreme wave manipulation, advancement of neurotechnology, and soft and stretchable electronics.
