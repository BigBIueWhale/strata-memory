If Apple built the 2.11 TiB machine — watts per served user vs GB200 NVL36

Same feat as the rest of this repo — hold Kimi K3 at 1M‑token full‑KV quality in 2.11 TiB — but built the way Apple would: LPDDR5X unified memory instead of a DDR5‑impersonation converter. Then the honest question: does it actually win on power?

Companion to kimi_k3_1m_full_quality.html (the 2.11 TiB build), kimi_k3_serving_economics.html (the per‑user KV math + NVL36), and strata_v_design.html (§12 energy‑per‑bit). Two pinned constraints from the brief: (1) the Apple compute die is identical to an RTX 5090 on prefill — not a real Apple part — and (2) Apple serves one running agent while NVL36 serves however many fit its VRAM (~165). First‑principles estimates, ±30%. Compiled 2026‑07.

The intuition — “Apple sips, NVIDIA guzzles” — is true in absolute watts and inverts the moment you divide by users. This document derives both numbers from the physics of where a joule actually goes, keeps prefill and decode strictly separate (they burn energy in different silicon), and does not let the Apple machine cheat with an efficiency lever the brief took away from it.

The answer, stated first

Per served user, at matched speed, NVL36 is more energy‑efficient — ~2.0× on prefill, ~1.5× on decode. Apple wins ~100× on absolute power, on cost, and on single‑user latency. They are optimized for different verbs.

NVIDIA is built to move bits fast for many; Apple is built to hold bits at low power for one. A 1M‑token conversation is prefill‑heavy (ingesting the context is an O(N²) compute mountain), so most of its joules land in exactly the phase where the brief’s “5090‑compute” clamp makes Apple ~2× worse. Apple’s genuinely beautiful LPDDR5X only wins the smaller (decode) slice — and even there only to a near‑tie.

01First principles — why anything here burns a joule, and when

Every watt in either machine is one of exactly four physical events. Naming them is the whole analysis, because two of them fire during prefill and two during decode, on different silicon — so a machine can win one phase and lose the other.

MechanismThe physicsPaid mostly during…Favors
Move a bit down a wire½·C·V² per transition — charge the wire’s capacitance C through voltage Vdecode (stream weights + KV)low V, short wire, few hops
Activate a DRAM rowdump a whole row into sense‑amps to read any of it (amortized when streaming)both~everyone (same 1T1C array)
Refresh the arrayleaky cells must be topped up every ~32–64 ms; scales with capacityalways — idle includedlow‑leakage DRAM (LPDDR)
One multiply‑accumulate∝ C·V² of the toggling transistors; FP4 « FP8 « FP16prefill (O(N²) attn + O(N·P) FFN)low V/f, wide silicon, low precision

Two consequences fall straight out, and they decide everything below:

  • The V² term is the master lever, and it splits by workload. Prefill energy lives in the compute V²; decode energy lives in the interface V². Different transistors, different voltages — so “which machine is more efficient” has two answers, not one.
  • Wide‑and‑slow beats narrow‑and‑fast, always. Raising clock f needs higher V, so dynamic power runs ~V²·f ≈ f³. Spreading the same throughput over 2× the silicon at ½ the clock costs far fewer joules per op. This is Apple’s real superpower — and §03 is where the brief confiscates it.

02What Apple would actually do — and the memory it would pick

Apple’s house style is not a DDR5 warehouse behind an impersonation PHY. It is unified LPDDR5X soldered next to the compute die — and from the ½CV² view that is the single best bit‑mover in the industry, on three axes at once:

So Apple’s answer to this whole repo is blunt: don’t impersonate GDDR7 over DDR5 at all — put the 2.11 TiB in LPDDR5X unified memory and read it directly. That is exactly the “co‑designed side‑port” your own strata_v_design.html §06 design‑in paradox already crowns as superior (it is what NVIDIA Grace does). Now watch the brief spend it.

03The clamp — the brief removes Apple’s best lever on purpose

Constraint (1): the Apple compute die is identical to an RTX 5090 on prefill. Read through §01’s second consequence, that is not a small stipulation — it is a surrender of Apple’s entire compute‑efficiency advantage.

  • Real Apple silicon is the world champion of wide‑and‑slow: giant dies at ~1 GHz and low voltage, riding the bottom of the V²f curve.
  • An RTX 5090 is the opposite operating point: a ~750 mm² die pushed to ~2.7 GHz at high voltage to hit peak clocks — paying the cubic power penalty for frequency.
  • Pinning “identical compute to a 5090” forces the Apple box onto the 5090’s voltage/clock point for the compute‑bound phase. Apple’s low‑V compute lever is switched off; only its low‑V memory lever survives.

The consequence is precise: Apple’s advantage is now confined to the bit‑movement term (decode), and denied in the MAC term (prefill). Constraint (2) — one agent vs ~165 — then removes its other structural lever, amortization. Both removals favor NVL36, and both are the brief’s own choices, not the hardware’s.

05Scenario A — maxed prefill (compute‑bound)

Ingesting a 1M‑token context is a MAC problem: energy = FLOPs × J/FLOP. The FLOPs are fixed by K3 and the context; the J/FLOP is where the machines diverge — and §03’s clamp is the whole story.

Prefill, maxedMachine powerUsersPower / served userPrefill speed / user
Apple box~720 W (compute 480 + mem 130 + platform 40, ÷0.9 PSU)1~720 W1.0× 5090

Verdict — NVL36 ~2.0× more efficient per user, at equal speed Prefill is MACs, and the datacenter part wins the V²f curve outright: B200 ≈ 7.5–9 TFLOPS/W FP4 vs the 5090’s ~3.5 TFLOPS/W — a ~2.5× J/FLOP gap that is entirely wide‑slow‑low‑V beating narrow‑fast‑high‑V. The Apple box, forced to be a 5090, eats that penalty; its excellent memory never enters the prefill equation. The ~720 vs 364 W gap is the 2.5× silicon‑efficiency gap, trimmed by Apple carrying less per‑user platform overhead.

06Scenario B — maxed decode (bandwidth‑bound)

Generation is a wire problem: energy = bytes‑moved × pJ/bit + refresh + whatever compute won’t power‑gate. Both machines read ~27 GB of active K3 weights (+ the 28 GiB MLA‑KV) per token, at matched per‑user bandwidth — so tok/s is equal and only the joules differ.

Decode, maxedMachine powerUsersPower / served userDecode speed / user
Apple box~430 W (mem maxed 180 + 5090 core idling 170 + platform 40, ÷0.9)1~430 W (best‑case gating ~300)1.79 TB/s → ~65 tok/s
NVL36~44 kW (~73% TDP; tensor cores idle, HBM maxed)~165~270 W1.75 TB/s → ~65 tok/s
Verdict — NVL36 ~1.5×, but it is close — and here’s the surprise LPDDR5X does not beat HBM3e per bit. HBM’s whole trick is 1,024+ pins at a low ~9 Gb/s each — low swing, short interposer reach — landing at ~3.5–4 pJ/bit, right alongside LPDDR5X. So Apple’s memory elegance buys parity, not a win, on the actual bits moved. What decides it is amortization: NVL36 spreads its CPUs, fabric, leakage, and static‑refresh across 165 users, while the Apple box makes one user carry a whole powered‑up 5090 core + the refresh of the full 2.11 TiB + the entire platform. Power‑gate the idle compute hard and Apple closes to a near‑tie (~300 vs ~270 W) — but it does not pull ahead.

In joules per decoded token per user (at ~65 tok/s): Apple ~6.6 J vs NVL36 ~4.1 J. The tok/s cancels, so the ratio is just the power ratio, ~1.6×.

07The verdict — and the inversion, in one table

“Apple sips, NVIDIA guzzles” is a pure absolute‑power artifact. It is completely true — and it flips under division, because NVIDIA’s 60 kW is 165‑way‑shared while the Apple box’s 0.5 kW serves one.

The “when” seals itA 1M‑token conversation is prefill‑heavy: ingesting 1M tokens is an O(N²) compute mountain, while decode is a per‑token trickle. So the workload spends the bulk of its joules in exactly the phase where the 5090‑clamp makes Apple ~2× worse. Apple’s LPDDR5X wins the smaller slice, to a tie. Only a short‑prompt / long‑output workload (decode‑dominated) would tilt back toward Apple — and even then, not past NVL36.

One line: NVIDIA is built to move bits fast for many; Apple is built to hold bits at low power for one. The 2.11 TiB feat is a capacity feat, so Apple’s LPDDR5X is the right memory — but the brief’s clamp (5090 compute) and its scope (one agent) hand NVIDIA the two levers, prefill efficiency and amortization, that decide watts‑per‑served‑user.

08Honest flags

  • ±30% throughout. The load‑bearing unknowns are the LPDDR5X static/refresh power at 2.11 TiB (scaled from Mac Studio, not measured at this capacity) and the two rack‑power split points (NVL36 near‑TDP at prefill, ~73% at decode). None of the verdicts flip inside the band: prefill stays a clear NVL36 win, decode stays NVL36‑wins‑to‑tie.
  • The 5090 core power split. 575 W TBP − ~95 W GDDR7 ≈ 480 W compute core (used at prefill); ~170 W assumed during memory‑bound decode (a stalled GPU still burns leakage + clocks + the light matmul‑vector). Aggressive power‑gating is the main way Apple’s decode number improves.
  • Decode tok/s uses the repo’s 27 GB/token weight‑read bound (~65 tok/s). Counting the 28 GiB MLA‑KV read per step at true 1M context pulls the realized rate toward ~30 tok/s — but equally for both machines, since per‑user bandwidth is matched, so the power ratio is unchanged.
  • Batching caveat, in NVL36’s favor and against it. Large‑batch MoE decode lets users share expert‑weight reads (helps NVL36’s effective throughput), but each user’s 28 GiB KV is private and re‑read every step (does not share) — at 1M context the KV term dominates, which is exactly why the per‑user‑bandwidth framing holds.
  • Apple runs 1 of a possible ~17. Per the brief. Let Apple fill its 2.11 TiB with ~17 agents and its fixed overhead amortizes ~17× — but each agent then shares the single 1.79 TB/s pool (~0.1 TB/s each → ~16× slower decode). That is the capacity‑vs‑bandwidth trade, not a free win; it changes the product, not just the price.
  • Cost is a different axis from power — and LPDDR5X is not the cheap memory. Two comparisons, kept apart. Vs NVL36’s HBM3e: LPDDR5X is cheaper per GB (~$12–21 vs ~$20–30+) and buyable in principle — so Apple wins $/user there, though the 2026 crunch has narrowed the gap (strata_v_design.html §13). Vs this repo’s DDR5‑5600 UDIMM build: LPDDR5X is not cheaper — mid‑2026 contract LPDDR5X (~$12–21/GB, surging ~90% QoQ on NVIDIA Grace/Rubin demand) runs parity‑to‑premium over commodity DDR5 UDIMM (~$13–15/GB) and climbs faster. Its edge over the DDR5 build is ~2.8× lower power + one‑hop integration — never price.
  • You cannot actually buy the LPDDR5X version. It is soldered, design‑in, contract‑allocation OEM silicon — no retail SKU, no swappable module, no 2.11 TiB kit on a shelf. The 45× DDR5 UDIMM build is the only one an ordinary buyer can assemble; the LPDDR5X path is the “you must be Apple/Grace” design‑in route (strata_v_design.html §06) — a real practical asymmetry, not a footnote.

Bottom line. Building the 2.11 TiB machine the Apple way — LPDDR5X unified memory — genuinely cuts the memory subsystem to ~1/3 of the STRATA‑V DDR5 converter’s (~175 W vs ~490 W), because 0.5 V on‑package point‑to‑point is the lowest‑energy way to move a bit. But with the compute clamped to an RTX 5090 and the machine serving one agent, Apple’s two decisive levers — low‑voltage compute and 165‑way amortization — are both switched off, so NVL36 wins watts‑per‑served‑user ~2.0× (prefill) and ~1.6× (decode), while Apple wins ~100× on absolute power, plus cost and single‑user QoS. The efficient‑Apple counterfactual is real — it just lands on the axes the brief did not ask about.
