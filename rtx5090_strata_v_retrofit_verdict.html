Can STRATA‑V retrofit a stock RTX 5090?

Whether the DDR5 memory‑virtualizer can replace a shipped 5090’s soldered GDDR7 and trick it into 2 TiB — at the same characteristics.

Companion to strata_v_design.html (the DDR5 virtualizer) and a device‑mode‑PHY signal‑integrity analysis this leans on. Verdict is protocol‑determined; NVIDIA’s GB202 controller/PCB/training design is not public and is not needed to reach it. Compiled 2026‑07‑18.

STRATA‑V works — behind a controller you own. This record answers the different question: can you take a finished, shipped RTX 5090, remove its 16 soldered GDDR7 devices, wire STRATA‑V in their place, and have the card believe it now has 2 TiB with a 5090’s exact behaviour? The answer is no, and it fails on four independent walls, any one of which is disqualifying.

Verdict

No — not on a stock 5090. Not at any effort.

A shipped GPU’s memory is defined by its controller, not its chips. The GB202 controller is hard‑configured for 32 GiB of GDDR7, and GDDR7 has no rank / chip‑select / capacity‑expansion signalling — so the card physically never issues an address past 32 GiB. Even the 32 GiB it can address it samples on a training‑frozen beat with no flow control, which a converter hop misses; and the 28 Gb/s PAM3 net cannot be routed off‑board to reach STRATA‑V at all. STRATA‑V is a custom‑controller design; the stock 5090 is the one thing it can’t be bolted onto — the same reason you can’t add RAM to a soldered phone.

01The exact question

Given a working STRATA‑V (the DDR5 memory‑virtualizer: ~18 MRDIMM channels aggregated by a converter hierarchy into one virtual 2.25 TiB / 1.79 TB/s device), and a retail RTX 5090: desolder the sixteen GDDR7 packages, connect STRATA‑V to the freed 512‑bit bus, and obtain a 5090 that reports and uses >2 TiB with unchanged latency/bandwidth/protocol. This is a retrofit of an immutable device — not a co‑design.

02The crux: whose controller is it?

Every earlier “yes” for STRATA‑V carried one precondition — you design the memory controller. That is the whole difference:

The memory a GPU has is a property of the controller that names and times it — not of whatever silicon happens to sit on the wires. Change the chips without changing the controller and the controller still lives in its old world.

03Wall A — Addressing (the fatal one)

Proof it is real: the GB202 controller is configured at boot for 16 × 2 GiB GDDR7 devices and emits bank/row/column addresses inside that geometry. Naming 2 TiB needs +6 address bits (235→241). In DDR‑family memory those bits arrive as extra ranks (chip‑select) or via an RCD — but GDDR7 has no rank, no chip‑select, and no capacity‑expansion mechanism; it deleted them for speed (confirmed against the Micron GDDR7 brief and JESD239 structure in the fast‑tier research). There is no bit pattern the 5090 can put on the bus that addresses the other ~2 TiB. The VBIOS, driver, and OS enumerate 32 GiB and never ask for more. A converter cannot present capacity through an interface that has no way to name it. This wall alone ends the retrofit.

04Wall B — Frozen timing / latency

Proof it is real: the controller trains its DQS‑gate and read eye against the chips, freezes the taps, and thereafter samples read data on an exact beat CL cycles after each READ — with no wait‑states and no flow control (the mechanism any device‑mode‑PHY emulator must live within). STRATA‑V’s read path is a pipeline — front deserialise → address translate → DDR5 access → reserialise → launch — which lands later than a native GDDR7 die. Late data on a frozen beat is silently latched garbage. Making a fabric pipeline land inside a foreign controller’s already‑frozen window is the frozen‑window launch problem, the deepest unsolved device‑mode‑PHY question — and inserting a DDR5 access makes it strictly worse.

05Wall C — Boot training

Proof it is real: before a single normal access, the 5090 runs its GDDR7 bring‑up — CA training, WCK2CK levelling, per‑DQ read/write training, per‑pin VREF, ZQ calibration — each step demanding a protocol‑exact response in hard real time. STRATA‑V’s front PHY would have to be a perfect GDDR7 device through all of it or the card does not POST. That is the exact‑training‑responder problem (a device‑mode PHY that answers every training step perfectly), and it must be flawless, not approximate.

06Wall D — Physical / signal integrity

Proof it is real: the GDDR7 bus is 28 Gb/s PAM3, point‑to‑point, on ultra‑short (≤~45 mm) length‑matched, low‑loss traces. You cannot desolder the chips and route those nets off‑board to an external STRATA‑V: a connector stub plus cable destroys a PAM3 eye that already has ~50% margin over two stacked sub‑eyes (the well‑documented absence of any deployable ≥2.4 GHz DDR probe front‑end). STRATA‑V’s front PHY would have to live in the exact GDDR7 BGA footprints, with identical trace lengths — i.e. become a device‑mode PHY in the DRAM’s place, which is precisely the full set of device‑mode‑PHY open problems, all unsolved.

07The one escape — and why it collapses

The only way to touch 2 TiB through a 32 GiB‑bound controller is to keep a 32 GiB window and page a larger backing store in and out of it. But nothing in the GDDR7 protocol lets the host request a page swap, so STRATA‑V would need a modified driver to issue swaps through a side channel (magic addresses). That is no longer transparent, the swaps run at DDR5‑copy speed (not “same characteristics”), and it still faces Walls B–D for even the 32 GiB window. It is software‑managed offloading with extra steps — which a stock 5090 can already do over PCIe today, more simply. It does not deliver a 2 TiB 5090.

08What integration would actually take — the Mellanox lesson

The Mellanox analogy points the right way if read correctly. NVIDIA’s Mellanox tie‑up was an acquisition (~2019, ~$6.9 B) of a networking company (InfiniBand / ConnectX), whose silicon was then designed into the platform. Nobody desoldered a shipped product. The transferable lesson is exactly the custom‑controller path:

Design‑in, not bolt‑onYour converter gets into an NVIDIA GPU the same way HBM, NVLink‑C2C, or a CXL controller does: the memory controller is co‑designed to speak to it. That means a partnership/IP deal (or acquisition) where the next silicon is built with STRATA‑V’s address map, relaxed‑but‑fixed timing, and deterministic backing in mind — the STRATA‑V we specified. It is never a retrofit of a finished card. Mellanox is the argument for the custom‑die route and against the stock‑5090 hack.

09Sourcing & scope

  • Design docs. NVIDIA does not publish the GB202 memory‑controller specification, PCB schematics, or GDDR7 training RTL — all proprietary/NDA. The authoritative GDDR7 standard, JEDEC JESD239, is member‑gated. None were downloadable; none are needed, because the verdict is fixed by the GDDR7 protocol (no capacity‑expansion signalling) and the shipped controller’s immutability, both established earlier in this project.
  • What we do have (in pdfs/ and images/): the Micron GDDR7 brief, the RTX PRO 6000 datasheet + Blackwell‑PRO architecture whitepaper, and the RTX 6000D clamshell teardown photos — enough to fix every GDDR7 fact this record relies on.
  • Scope. This concerns a stock 5090 retrofit only. The custom‑controller STRATA‑V (a die you design) is unaffected and remains valid.

Bottom line. A finished GPU’s memory is set by its controller, and the 5090’s is frozen at 32 GiB of GDDR7 — an interface with no way to name more capacity, no slack to absorb a converter’s delay, a boot‑time training gate, and a 28 Gb/s PAM3 net that can’t leave the board. STRATA‑V’s home is a controller you own; you get it into NVIDIA silicon by design‑in, not by desoldering a card. Negative result, recorded.
