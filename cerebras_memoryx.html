Cerebras MemoryX — cheap DDR, decoupled from the wafer

The one shipping architecture built on STRATA-V’s thesis — commodity DRAM capacity split from expensive compute — and why it’s the opposite mechanism, which is exactly what makes it a clean proof-point.

Compiled 2026-07 from Cerebras primary disclosures (weight-streaming whitepaper, Hot Chips 33/34/36, IEEE Micro, CS-2/WSE-3/CS-3 datasheets, SEC Form S-1), Cerebras press/blog, and third-party analysis (The Next Platform, ServeTheHome, SemiAnalysis, IEEE Spectrum). Vendor claims flagged; confirmed-vs-reported noted. ~90 source artifacts and a full manifest in pricing_evidence/cerebras/.

Cerebras is the strongest real-world evidence that the STRATA-V thesis is right — at hyperscale, you buy capacity with cheap commodity DRAM and decouple it from the compute you can’t afford to over-provision. Their MemoryX appliance does exactly that. But they reach it from the far side of the memory hierarchy: not by making DRAM impersonate fast memory, but by removing the random-access contract entirely and streaming weights over a network. Reading how — and where it breaks — both validates the economics and confirms the niche STRATA-V occupies is empty.

Verdict, split cleanly

The economics is proven and shipping. The mechanism is the opposite of ours — and it abandons external DRAM the moment latency matters.

Proven: Cerebras trains 24-trillion-parameter models against a commodity-DDR5 memory box (Sandia, Dec 2024) and shares one weight copy across many wafers — capacity scaled independently of compute, at 82 TB / 64-system scale in production. Opposite: the wafer has no memory interface at all; weights are pushed to it as a scheduled dataflow stream, never read like memory. The tell: when Cerebras built latency-critical inference, they threw external DRAM out and went back to on-wafer SRAM — which is precisely the regime STRATA-V’s bus-level impersonation targets.

01What MemoryX actually is

MemoryX is an external appliance that holds a model’s weights and optimizer state off the wafer and streams them in on demand. It is active memory — it also computes the weight update between iterations — and it attaches over standard datacenter Ethernet, not a memory bus — the wafer’s attach pipe is 12×100 GbE in both generations; only the cluster fabric (the SwarmX switches) moved to 400G/800G for CS-3. The wafer itself has no DRAM controller and no memory PHY; its only external pipe is ~1.2 Tb/s of system I/O.

02Why comparatively slow external DRAM doesn’t starve the wafer

This is the crux, and it’s where the architecture diverges hardest from ours. Cerebras does not try to deliver VRAM-class bandwidth from DRAM. It restructures the workload so it doesn’t need to:

  • Weight streaming. Weights live in MemoryX and are streamed layer-by-layer onto the wafer, used, and discarded — “the weights are never stored on the system, not even temporarily.” Only activations stay resident, in the wafer’s 40/44 GB of SRAM.
  • Reuse hides the gap. Each streamed FP16 weight triggers compute across a batch of hundreds of thousands of resident tokens — arithmetic intensity of order 105–106 FLOP/byte. That is how ~150 GB/s of egress feeds a multi-PFLOPS wafer; the high-intensity operand traffic never leaves SRAM.
  • SwarmX shares one copy. The broadcast-reduce fabric sends one weight copy out and reduces gradients in-fabric on the way back, so a single MemoryX store feeds any number of wafers — “a single MemoryX unit can be used to target any number of CS-2 systems.” Capacity and compute scale on independent axes.
  • Latency is engineered away. Streaming is pipelined under compute; the round-trip to MemoryX is fully hidden as long as a layer’s compute exceeds it. The only synchronization point is the end-of-iteration weight update — performed inside MemoryX.
The load-bearing distinctionThe wafer never issues a “memory read” to MemoryX. It receives a self-describing, compiler-scheduled tensor stream. Cerebras solved capacity-decoupling above the memory interface — by deleting random access — whereas STRATA-V solves it at the interface, by emulating random access so faithfully the engine can’t tell. Same goal; opposite layer of the stack.

03The DDR verdict, kept honest

Is it “actual DDR”? Yes — and the record is precise about it:

  • CS-2 era: an explicit DRAM + NAND-flash hybrid (verbatim in three primary docs). ServeTheHome’s Hot Chips 2023 read of the internals: ~12 MemoryX nodes, up to ~1 TB DRAM + ~500 TB flash each, 32-core CPUs, 100 GbE. The DRAM is the streaming tier; flash is the capacity tier (Cerebras never assigns the roles explicitly, but the layer-order access pattern is what makes a flash tier tolerable).
  • CS-3 era: named outright as commodity DDR5 in ordinary 1U servers — the 55 TB Sandia unit was “procured and configured in mere days.” The capacity tier is now off-the-shelf DIMMs, not a custom memory device.
The over-claim to avoidNo Cerebras document states the DRAM technology per SKU, and the SEC S-1 never says “DDR” at all. “1.2 PB of DDR5” is not supported — the petabyte-class SKUs almost certainly keep a flash tier behind the DRAM. The defensible statement is: MemoryX is a commodity-DDR5-based appliance, with flash behind it in the largest historical configs.

04Marketing vs. what shipped

Worth internalizing before citing any headline number: the famous 2021 “2.4 PB / 120 trillion parameters” was an architecture envelope that never shipped at petabyte scale. The real, documented builds are one to three orders smaller:

  • Shipped MemoryX SKUs in the CS-2 era: 1.5 TB and 12 TB.
  • Largest disclosed real deployment: Condor Galaxy-1 = 82 TB across 64 CS-2s (386 Tb/s fabric, ~$100M).
  • Cleanest single proof-point: Sandia’s 55 TB commodity-DDR5 box feeding 1→16 CS-3s with zero code change (15.3× on 16 systems).
  • CS-3 re-based the ceiling to 1.2 PB / 24T params (~50 bytes/param — sized for full training state, not raw weights).

Independent analysts flag the 2,048-system / 256-exaFLOP cluster as catalog-only — ServeTheHome: “this seems to be a theoretical Cerebras CS-3 cluster at this point”; IEEE Spectrum: “nothing quite that big is in the works.” [vendor envelope vs. shipped reality — flagged]

05The tell — where Cerebras abandons external DRAM

The most useful finding for STRATA-V is what Cerebras stopped doing. Their 2024 inference product — the latency-critical regime — does not use MemoryX at all:

  • Weights and KV cache go SRAM-resident on the wafer; models too big for one wafer are split at layer boundaries across multiple CS-3s (4× WSE-3 = 176 GB of SRAM to hold a 70B model).
  • Cerebras’s own words: storing the model on-chip “eliminat[es] the need for external memory and for the slow lanes linking external memory to compute.”
  • The SEC S-1 files MemoryX strictly under “training parameter storage”; for inference, “model parameters are stored in on-chip memory for ultra-low latency.”
Why this vindicates the designCerebras’s own product line demonstrates that fabric-attached DRAM streaming is a training technique that does not survive the latency-critical path. When tokens must come out fast, they pay for on-wafer SRAM by the wafer rather than stream from DRAM. STRATA-V attacks exactly that gap from the other side: keep commodity DRAM’s depth and serve it under a native, low-latency memory interface — the thing Cerebras’s architecture structurally cannot do without abandoning DRAM.

06Two extremes of the memory hierarchy — 44 GB of on-wafer SRAM vs. commodity DDR5

Cerebras’s most advanced chip, the WSE-3 (2024, TSMC 5 nm, 46,225 mm², 4 trillion transistors, 900,000 cores), carries 44 GB of on-wafer SRAM (WSE-2 had 40 GB) at a headline 21 PB/s of bandwidth. That SRAM and the DDR5 STRATA-V fronts are the two opposite corners of the memory hierarchy — and the contrast is the whole reason each architecture is shaped the way it is.

The giga-chad line, both waysSRAM’s flex: one WSE-3 moves 21 PB/s — ~10,000× a whole box of DDR5 and ~12,000× an RTX 5090’s 1.79 TB/s — and can re-read its entire contents ~477,000 times per second. DDR5’s flex: a single $200 DDR5 DIMM (48 GiB) out-capacities the entire most-advanced-chip-ever’s 44 GB of SRAM — and does it at ~1/1,000th the $/GB. On-wafer SRAM is the widest, fastest, shallowest, most expensive memory that exists; DDR5 is deep, cheap, and “merely” VRAM-adequate.

This single table explains both architectures. Cerebras spends a whole wafer to get 44 GB at 21 PB/s — so fast per byte that they must stream weights through it from cheap DRAM, because 44 GB can’t hold a frontier model. STRATA-V goes the other way: it doesn’t need 21 PB/s — a 5090 asks for only 1.79 TB/s — so 45 commodity DDR5 sticks deliver that bandwidth and 2.11 TiB of depth for ~$8k. The bandwidth-per-byte gap (~540,000×) is exactly why Cerebras streams and STRATA-V doesn’t have to.

07Can you NVLink the wafers together?

The obvious rescue for “44 GB isn’t enough” is NVIDIA’s own answer to the same wall: pool it. NVLink + NVSwitch weld 72 Blackwell GPUs into one domain where any GPU fires loads, stores, and atomics straight into any other’s HBM — “a single, massive GPU” with ~13.4 TB of shared fast memory at 130 TB/s. So — can you NVLink Cerebras wafers into one giant fast-memory pool?

The answer

No. And not for want of trying — on-wafer SRAM is the one memory tier physics forbids you to pool.

There is no NVLink for wafer SRAM: none shipped, none announced, none coherent even as a goal. What Cerebras actually runs between wafers is RDMA over Ethernet — and for inference it moves only activations wafer-to-wafer (Hot Chips 2024, verbatim: “<5 µs latency, 4 hops, ~100 Gb/s, <10% of the pipe”). That is message-passing DMA, not a load/store into a neighbor’s memory. One wafer never sees another wafer’s SRAM.

The law — poolability = local bandwidth ÷ the fastest off-die link you can build

Whether a memory tier can be pooled at all reduces to a single ratio: how far remote access falls below local. A few× and “one big shared pool” is honest; a thousand× and it’s a lie.

The 340×-short punchlineTo pool WSE-3 SRAM at NVLink’s honest “within ~4.4×” bar, each wafer would have to emit ~38 Pb/s. The best photonic interposer ever built — Lightmatter’s M1000, at a record 114 Tb/s — supplies 1/340th of that; Cerebras’s shipping wafer supplies 1/32,000th. SRAM’s entire value is locality; a remote wafer’s SRAM reached over any cable isn’t fast memory anymore — it’s slow, absurdly expensive remote memory. No link, optical or otherwise, closes a gap like that.

The clincher: the two teams who did unify addressing still refused to pool SRAM

This isn’t only arithmetic. The two architectures that actually built a flat address space across the die boundary chose not to share the fast memory anyway:

  • Tesla Dojo built a flat address space with hardware DMA into any endpoint (TCP-over-Ethernet cast into silicon) and a mere 2:1 off-tile bandwidth cliff — and still kept each tile’s 11 GB of SRAM “private,” pooling only the DRAM behind it. (Dojo was disbanded Aug 2025; a “Dojo 3” restarted Jan 2026.)
  • Groq “pools” 220 MiB × up to 10,440 chips into ~2 TB of “logically shared, physically distributed SRAM” — but only by compiling away all dynamism: the hardware is forbidden from ever asserting backpressure. It’s a compile-time fiction, not a fabric; the physical cliff is still ~1,600×.

Everyone who can pool, pools the tier below the fast one. The one company selling genuinely memory-semantic optical pooling — Celestial AI — was absorbed by Marvell (closed Feb 2026), which promptly joined NVLink Fusion; and it pooled the DRAM/HBM tier, never SRAM. Cerebras’s own $45M DARPA co-packaged-optics program (with Ranovus) widens the streaming pipe by ~100× — more MemoryX reach, not a pooling fabric. Even the speculative WSE-4 fix (§10) is shared DRAM trays, not shared SRAM.

Where STRATA-V lands, honestly: in the DRAM free-trade zone with everyone else — but attacking the frozen native interface of a merchant GPU instead of a co-designed fabric. Above the SRAM boundary you stream or pipeline; below it you pool or impersonate. That cell — impersonate × unmodified-GPU host — is the one nobody occupies (see prior_art.html).

08How Cerebras runs a huge model — and what GLM-5.2 costs

NVIDIA runs a frontier model by pooling: an NVL72 puts ~13.4 TB of HBM in one load/store domain, and the whole model plus everyone’s KV cache lives in it. Cerebras can’t pool (§07) — so how does it actually serve, say, GLM-5.2? The honest answer is a first-principles wafer count, and it is steep.

The mechanism is a layer-partitioned, SRAM-resident pipeline: the model is sliced across wafers by layer, weights and KV cache live in on-wafer SRAM, and only activations cross between wafers (RDMA over Ethernet, <5 µs/hop). MemoryX never enters the inference path (§05). Capacity for a big model is bought three ways: more wafers, the model’s own quantization (Kimi-K2.6 is served at native INT4, Qwen3-Coder-480B at FP8 — the “16-bit-always” era is over), and prefill offload (AWS Bedrock runs prefill on Trainium and hands the decode to CS-3).

The MoE residency trapGLM-5.2 is a 753B-parameter mixture-of-experts (256+1 experts per layer, only ~40B active per token). You might hope to pay for the active slice — but on an SRAM-resident engine any token can route to any expert, so all 753B must be resident at once. Memory bills the total; compute bills only the active 5.3%. MoE saves flops, not footprint — which is exactly what makes wafer-scale serving of big MoEs so brutal.

The wafer count, from first principles

WSE-3 = 44 GB SRAM/wafer, of which ~35 GB is usable (79.5% packing, calibrated on Cerebras’s one disclosed mapping — Llama-70B on 4 wafers). GLM-5.2 at full native 1,048,576-token context:

  • Weights (BF16): 753B × 2 B = 1,507 GB → 1,507 / 35 = 43 wafers.
  • KV cache (SRAM-resident, MLA): ~95 GB per 1M-token user ≈ 2.8 wafers/user. Eight concurrent full-context streams → +23 wafers.
  • Total ≈ 66 CS-3 systems.

The trade, stated plainly: ~45× the capex and ~800× less long-context concurrency per dollar — bought for ~10× the per-user speed. Cerebras wins where latency compounds (agentic loops, long reasoning chains) and loses on bulk tokens and on packing many long-context users per dollar — which is why it sells tokens ($0.25–$12 per M) and amortizes the silicon across a fleet rather than selling you the box. It is also how Cerebras crossed the trillion-parameter line without pooling: Kimi-K2.6 (1.06T) fits on ~18 wafers only because it is stored at native INT4, with prefill given to Trainium and just the memory-bound decode kept on-wafer. The endgame is the STRATA-V shape at datacenter scale — an expensive fast-memory decode engine, fronted by cheap-memory everything-else.

The million-dollar test

You can buy Cerebras chips and pay a million dollars for every additional 17.5 GB of SRAM 🤣. Or pay a million dollars to NVIDIA for every additional 3.2 TB of VRAM (a B200-based NVLink supercomputer). Or pay just ~$25K for 2.11 TiB of DDR5 💀.

That is ~$57,000/GB vs ~$310/GB vs ~$11/GB — three orders of magnitude from the fast corner of the memory hierarchy to the cheap one, and the RTX 5090 can’t tell the difference.

10The convergence worth watching

Analysts now argue the WSE’s compute-to-SRAM ratio is “wrong for low-latency inference” (The Next Platform, 2026) — 44 GB/wafer forces multi-wafer serving for anything much above ~20B params in FP16. The speculated WSE-4 remedy is telling: “optical links coming out of the wafer to shared DRAM memory trays to significantly expand the MemoryX capacity.” That is Cerebras drifting toward natively-attached external DRAM — the very problem STRATA-V attacks from the interface side. 11Honest flags

  • Confirmed vs. reported.   • Qualcomm is not a MemoryX datapoint. The 2024 “train-on-Cerebras, infer-on-Qualcomm AI 100 Ultra” partnership uses Qualcomm’s 128 GB LPDDR4x — a conventional DRAM-behind-NPU design, unrelated to MemoryX; the route vanished from later messaging.
  • Numbers moved. 386 vs 388 Tb/s (CG-1); “1.5/12/1200 TB” (PR) vs “24/36/120/1200 TB” (same-day blog) vs “12 to 1,200 TB” (datasheet). Cite with a date.
  • Stealth / roadmap. WSE-4 memory is unannounced; CS-3-era per-SKU DRAM technology is not fully published. Absence of a public teardown of a MemoryX enclosure is itself a gap.

Bottom line. MemoryX is the clearest proof that cheap commodity DDR5, decoupled from a very expensive compute engine, is the right way to buy capacity at scale — Cerebras trains 24T-param models against exactly that. It is simultaneously the clearest proof that their way of doing it — stream over a fabric, restructure the workload, tolerate latency — breaks in the latency-critical path, where they retreat to on-wafer SRAM bought by the wafer. The economics vindicates STRATA-V; the mechanism leaves the native-interface-impersonation niche genuinely unoccupied. Two opposite corners of the memory hierarchy, aimed at the same wall.
